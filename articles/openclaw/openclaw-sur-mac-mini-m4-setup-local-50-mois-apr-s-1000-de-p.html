Chapter 1: Meeting Doraemon & The "Mum-Proof" Setup

My AI company lives inside a 5-inch silver box. I call him Doraemon. He’s an M4 Mac Mini with 16GB of RAM that sits next to my monitor and never sleeps.
Most people think you need a complex "Cloud VPS" to run autonomous agents. They’re wrong. Local hardware is the only way to get zero-latency execution without the monthly "server tax." If you have an M4 Mac Mini or any idle laptops, you have everything you need to build your own OpenClaw.
Wait, what if I don't have a Mac Mini? A VPS is still a great option if you have zero hardware available. If you're traveling or want your Openclaw in the cloud, a basic VPS can host your agents 24/7. Just remember: it's a monthly fee vs. the one-time cost of owning your hardware.
The Brain: Free Trial vs. Deep Focus
You have two ways to power Openclaw mind:
  • The "Free" Start: Sign up for Google Cloud and get $300 in free credits. This lets you run models like Gemini 2, 2.5, or 3 Flash/Pro for free. Warning: The rate limits are very awkward and you will run out of "juice" quickly when your agents start working in the background.
  • The Deep Focus Choice: If you want to avoid rate limit headaches, I recommend MiniMax M2.5. I chose the Max Plan for $50/month, which gives you 1,000 prompts every 5 hours. For beginners, even the $10–$20 plans are enough to start.

Step 1: Installing the Engine
Open your Terminal (Cmd + Space, type "Terminal") and install Node.js and the OpenClaw framework:
bash
brew install node
npm install -g openclaw@latest
Step 2: Onboarding Openclaw
Type following command in your terminal and follow the prompts.
bash
openclaw onboard --install-daemon

  1. Open your bot conversation on Telegram.

The Payoff: When your phone buzzes with a message from your Mac Mini, you’ll realize the game has changed. You don't just have a chatbot; you have a teammate.

Chapter 2: The 9-Agent Disaster & The $1,000 Heartbeat Trap

I’ll admit it: I got too excited. I was 25, I had Doraemon (my M4 Mac Mini) running, and I felt like a god. I built a "dream team" of 9 AI agents, including OSIRIS, Grace & Ada, and Aether.
It was beautiful until I woke up to a financial nightmare.
The "Silent Bank Account Killer"
The problem started with the Google token and the "Heartbeat". The heartbeat is the pulse that makes an agent autonomous. It tells them to wake up and check for tasks. With 9 agents all pulsing 24/7, I was hitting the API every few seconds.
  • The Token Burn: I obliterated my limits in Google AI Studio.
  • The Ban: I was banned from Antigravity despite paying for Pro.
  • The Cost: Between the bans and fallbacks, I burned over 200 bucks within 3 days.
The Solution: Moving Heartbeats to LM Studio
To stop the bleeding, I moved the Heartbeats off the paid APIs and onto LM Studio, running locally on Doraemon.
If you want to run heartbeats locally on an M4 Mac Mini (16GB), here is the performance breakdown I found:
  • Qwen 2.5 (3-4B): The "Speed Demon." Insane response times, but can be "stupid" for complex logic.
  • Gemma 2 (3-4B): Rock-solid stability.
  • Gemma 3 4B (21 sec): Incredible at instruction following for its size.
  • Qwen 3 4B (30 sec): The "Agentic King." It's the most stable for tool-use and routing tasks.
The "Flavor" Guide: Think of these 3B-4B models as roughly equivalent to GPT-4o mini in terms of their ability to handle basic "routing" and "checking" tasks. They aren't as smart as the full Claude 3.5 Sonnet, but for a heartbeat (which just needs to check if there is work to do), they are perfect and totally free to run.
The Great Reset
I realized that complexity is the enemy of execution. I burned the 9-agent team down and moved to an Elite Squad of 2. By running the heartbeats locally in LM Studio and the "heavy thinking" through MiniMax, I created a system that is faster, cleaner, and doesn't bankrupt me while I sleep.

Chapter 3: Execution Over Tokens — The "Freshman" Workforce

The "Great Reset" wasn't just about saving money; it was about reclaiming my time. I was tired of being a "token accountant" for Google AI Studio and fighting bans. I’m 25, I’m building a startup, and I need a system that works as hard as I do.
The $50 Power Move
I fired the "Pro" tools and moved to a MiniMax M2.5 Max Plan for $50/month. This was the game-changer:
  • Massive Volume: 1,000 prompts every 5 hours.
  • Claude Code Integration: I routed MiniMax directly into Claude Code and OpenClaw. Now, I have a world-class coding agent running 24/7 on a high-volume model that doesn't quit.
Strategic Balance: The Hybrid Engine
This is the most important lesson: MiniMax is the Brain, but Local is the Battery. You need to balance your workload based on agent intensity:
  • Low Agent Count: If you only have 2 agents, let MiniMax handle everything. It’s fast, smart, and handles the "heavy thinking" without breaking a sweat.
  • Scaling Up (The Emergency Valve): When you add more agents, the "Heartbeats" (constant check-ins) will start eating your rate limits. This is when you offload those boring, repetitive pings to LM Studio running locally on Openclaw for $0. Use the local models (like Qwen 3 or Gemma 3) as a secret valve to keep your MiniMax prompts reserved for actual coding and logic.
The Elite Squad: The "Freshman" Rule
I stripped my 9-agent "dream team" down to a 2-agent Elite Squad running on Openclaw. But the secret isn't just having fewer agents—it's how you manage them. I use the Freshman Rule:
  1. One Task Per Agent: I only allow them to do one task at a time. If you give an AI five jobs, it fails at four.
  2. Treat Them Like Freshmen: Fundamentally, you have to treat these agents like a freshman who doesn't know anything at all. Don't assume they remember your project's history. Give them clear, ground-up instructions every single time.
Why This Is the Future for Solo Founders
