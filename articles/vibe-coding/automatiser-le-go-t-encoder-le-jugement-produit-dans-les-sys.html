Taste is the thing you should be automating
There's a popular narrative right now that when AI does all the coding, "taste" is what remains. Taste is the last human skill. The irreducible thing. I think that gets it backwards. Taste is exactly what you should be encoding into the system.
Taste might be the thing AI applies best.
Software today is a working artifact improved piece by piece by humans. Humans prioritize, plan, execute. Agents don't change this. They make it faster. You're still handing tasks between people, or between people-shaped agents. A powerful software development agent managed by a human is like an exoskeleton.
Orchestrating many agents doesn't change the paradigm either. It's a faster assembly line. The bottleneck is now obviously not execution speed. At Factory, for a few months now every engineer has been able to ship thousands of lines of self-consistent code changes daily. But having that power doesn't mean that's the right way to ship. Velocity is now decided by accurately picking what to build next, what matters, what's good enough to ship. People are calling this taste. And right now, taste lives in someone's head, applied inconsistently, bottlenecked on their calendar. Thought leaders will tell you that only some people can ever have this taste.
Modern product development is deeply reactive. New models drop weekly, competitors ship overnight, customer needs shift based on capabilities that didn't exist a month ago. But when taste is a person, reactivity is bounded by their bandwidth. They can only re-triage so fast before quality degrades. A system with encoded prioritization can instantly re-triage when the signal environment changes. Human-bottlenecked taste breaks down precisely when the environment is most chaotic, which is precisely when good judgment matters most.
The principles of taste are actually well known. Build things people need. Fix issues for your users. Build features your customers ask for. Remove features they don't use. Design interactions that feel obvious. Think about your problem from first principles. Question assumptions. Understand your users. These principles are difficult for humans to consistently adhere to. But your AI systems can be taught to rigorously apply them.
So you must automate taste. You write down your prioritization rules. You define what "good" looks like as review criteria. You formalize the things you care about. You institute machine + AI quality control and validation. The system will apply your taste more consistently than you ever did. Not because AI has better judgment in any single moment, but because it doesn't get tired on Friday afternoon. It doesn't have recency bias. It doesn't forget the thing you said mattered last quarter.
Humans have great taste in bursts. AI can be designed to have decent taste, constantly, across every decision, at 3am on a Sunday. And the comparison is not like one person being more consistent. It's about thirty people applying the same judgment simultaneously. When every pod, every sprint, every builder operates on implicit and divergent standards, quality is random. Encoded taste means the whole organization is executing against the same criteria at the same time. At scale, consistency wins.
An autonomous product engineering system might look something like this:
1. Ideas flowing in as structured inputs from signals like telemetry, customer feedback, errors, and simulated agent feedback
2. Prioritization is encoded in an explicit framework
3. Execution and validation fully automated by software development agents
4. Approval gates enforced programmatically 5. Deployed software generates the signals that restart the loop
A concrete version: an open source repo where signals from telemetry, GitHub issues, and user feedback collect in lightweight infrastructure defined in the repo itself. A triage agent processes these against the encoded prioritization framework and manages work items in a project management tool. Coding agents pick up prioritized tasks, generate implementations, and loop in humans for visibility. AI code review, security scanning, and QA validation run automatically. Merge and release are automated and scheduled.
Every agent run is traced. When an agent fails or gets rejected at the gate, that trajectory is recorded and patterns emerge: which tasks are hard, which instructions are ambiguous. Those patterns feed back into the signal layer: documentation and review criteria get refined. Agent effectiveness and agent readiness become measurable, improvable properties of the codebase itself.
Maintainers rarely touch code. They govern a meta layer: documents defining purpose and priorities, linters, typecheckers, AI workflows that enforce quality standards. They look at agent traces and ask: where is the system failing, and what's preventing it from being more autonomous? They make the taste sharper.
We become shepherds, not builders.
This doesn't work for everything. Zero-to-one product decisions, strategic pivots, etc. - times where there's no signal yet: these still need a human with conviction making a call. The system is bad at "we should exist in this market" and good at "given that we exist in this market, here's what to build next." The further you get from established signal, the more you need a person. But most software work isn't zero-to-one. Most of it is the long tail of improvements, fixes, and features where taste is just disciplined application of known principles. That's where this system excels.
The visual everyone should have of modern software delivery is a line of humans handing things to each other. AI-assisted development is the same line with exoskeletons. What you want is a factory. Humans maintain the factory. The factory builds the software. And the factory's taste - its encoded judgment about what to build and what's good enough - is what requires the most human attention.
This is how humans stay involved. When you stop spending your time on execution and start building the systems that govern execution, you can aim at things that were previously impossible. The global financial system, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, all contemporary operating systems, the internet itself: these are total feats of human engineering, and they were all built without this new technology. The ceiling on what we could build was always set by how many people you could coordinate and how fast they could move. That ceiling is about to disappear.
Stop protecting taste as the last human skill. We are moving up from brick laying as a profession. We can now build cathedrals.
