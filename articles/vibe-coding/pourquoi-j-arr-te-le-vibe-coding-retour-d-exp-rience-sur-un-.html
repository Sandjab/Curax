Stopping Vibe-Coding for Good
I am writing this post from a flight without internet, and these three hours feel like the longest without tokenmaxxing, or for the lack of a better word, vibe-coding.
I do not like using the term “agentic engineering” because that phrase reminds me of how I recently took a screenshot from iMessage of a bug and pasted it in Cursor with the prompt, “pls fix”.
A bug that should not have occurred in the first place because I was busy vibing on my open-sourced project, App Store Connect CLI.
The project has grown bigger than I imagined, but I am not proud that I only know about it at a surface level. Yes, I know the commands and how to use it without AI. No, I cannot code in Go.
Does it matter that I should know how to code? I think so.
The models have gotten much better over the year, and Go is one of the languages that they are usually strong with. There are a lot of open-sourced CLI that I took learnings from, especially gh cli as the prime example. But I wanted to speed run this project, cover the breadth, and then refactor the AI debt every weekend. The project is almost 300K lines of code, but I find the number meaningless to keep track of because the language is verbose, and the real flex in the AI slop world is to achieve in lesser number of lines of code, and decrease the surface area of errors.
The vibe-coding approach worked fine when it was just another folder that existed on GitHub, but now it is much more. The way things are accelerating and folks curious about a modern equivalent of fastlane, I cannot vibe-code this project, for good.
Creating the MVP in an hour for TestFlight feedback and crash logs was fine, but running parallel agents on everything under the Sun of App Store Connect API was a mistake. Not because it does not work well, but because the commands are more scattered than I accept in a pre-AI world. And refactoring them into proper commands means deprecating them instead of just deleting them, if it was just a hobby project lying around. That is why I am hesitant to do a 1.0 release as it is not up to my taste, and I went with the flow vibing until I realised it later.
My friend reached out to me for parity to “match”, one of the prominent tools of fastlane. The model I am using recommended 5-6 months for full parity, and 7-8 weeks for the MVP with one strong Go engineer. I know it is overestimating, and I can get it to have the MVP done over the night vibing on cloud agent(s). But I want to take my time to understand how it works, what approach will be better, the pros/cons of it, how should the “agent” first approach apply here, and the best testing suite for covering edge cases for different organisations.
And this definitely is not vibe-coding anymore because I am invested in the process as much as the agent.
The only difference is that I am still not the one writing code. I do not want to write code manually anymore. I am okay reviewing the code, the flow, make the agent make me understand the trade-offs it takes, walk me through how it would use these sets of commands, try to optimise and make fewer tool calls, and when it is up to the mark, release it for folks to try.
And I will go back and follow the same procedure for the rest of the existing commands. This will slow me down significantly in the near future, but this is what I envision the future as. Be hyper focused and work with the agents, and let it take care of the implementation and testing process, to give me an almost bug-free output. I use the word almost because no matter how much we try, we will miss out on edge cases that we did not anticipate.
There are founders that I had conversations with who are willing to put my CLI into their release pipeline that will be across *hundreds of millions* of devices.
And I have to stop vibe-coding for good.
